Lead Researcher

Dr Trisalyn Nelson
Lansdowne Research Chair in Spatial Analysis & Associate Professor BSc (University of Victoria), MSc (University of Victoria), PhD (Wilfrid Laurier University)

Physical and human environments are changing faster than ever and many pressing questions require characterization of change over space and through time. New spatial technologies (e.g., Satellites, GPS, and Google Maps) are providing opportunities to investigate spatial-temporal processes but methods are required to best utilize data. Generally, Dr. Nelsonâ€™s research develops and uses spatial and spatial-temporal analyses to address applied questions in ecology, forestry, and health. Dr. Nelson is particularly interested in developing approaches to quantify space-time change in landscapes (i.e., biodiversity) and phenomena that use landscapes (i.e., wildlife).

Graduate Students

  • Shanley Thompson, PhD Candidate
    BSc (Queen's University), MSc (University of British Columbia)

    Global Biodiversity Mapping and Monitoring with Remote Sensing

    Generally, my interests lie in mapping and monitoring land use, ecosystems, species distributions and habitats, within the contexts of environmental conservation and management. Past research and work experience has taken me from British Columbia, to Saskatchewan, to the Canadian High Arctic, and to the Ecuadorian Andes. My PhD research builds on these experiences, and involves using a variety of remotely sensed data with spatial and statistical modeling to derive baseline biodiversity maps at several spatial scales. My research will help fill gaps in the knowledge of spatial distribution of biodiversity (species and ecosystems). This type of knowledge is essential when assessing risks to biodiversity from natural and anthropogenic disturbances.

  • Jessica Fitterer, PhD Candidate
    BSc (University of Victoria), MSc (University of Victoria)

    Spatial Analysis of alcohol outlet density and violence in British Columbia

    My MSc studies allowed me the opportunity to explore terrestrial ecosystem and species modelling in which I utilized Earth observation and terrain indices to develop a dynamic ecosystems model for British Columbia and created predictive breeding bird diversity models to support species management. During my PhD studies I will be turning my attention to researching the spatial relationship between substance use and criminal offenses over the province of British Columbia. Specifically, I intend to establish if an increased density of liquor establishments has an influence on the rate of violence in an area, and possibly nuisance crimes, to inform policy for public health and safety.

  • Mathieu Bourbonnais, PhD Candidate
    BA (University of Alberta), BSc (Honours, University of Victoria), MSc (University of Victoria)

    Quantifying and Predicting Wildlife Movement

    Generally, my research interests lie in the application and development of spatial methods in order to answer a wide variety of ecological questions. My Honours research examined the impacts of mountain pine beetle infestation on spatial patterns of forest fire ignition and size, while my MSc quantified impacts of habitat conditions and human disturbance on long-term stress and health of grizzly bears in Alberta, Canada. Working with the Foothills Research Institute Grizzly Bear Project, my PhD research will focus on the development of novel methods and tools to quantify and predict wildlife movement using high resolution telietry data. The goal is to develop new applications to help assess the impacts of human disturbance and habitat conditions on wildlife movement in order to better inform management and conservation efforts.

  • Robin Kite, MSc Candidate
    BSc(University of Alberta), BSc(Honours,University of Victoria)

    Intra-annual Habitat Selection by Grizzly Bears

    My research concentration during my MSc will be the application of spatial-temporal analysis methods to quantify intra-annual variation in grizzly bear home ranges in relation to seasonally abundant food resources in the foothills region of Alberta. In order to facilitate conservation efforts in a region supporting grizzly bear populations, resource extraction, and recreational activities, it is important to understand what factors, natural or anthropogenic, influence grizzly bear habitat selection.

  • Michael Branion-Calles, MSc Candidate
    BSc(with Distinction, University of Victoria)

    Spatial analysis of health indicators and radon gas in British Columbia

    My research interests lie at the intersection between public health and spatial sciences. During my MSc research I will be using spatial analysis methods to better understand the relationship between health outcomes and radon gas concentrations in homes in British Columbia. Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that can seep into homes from the surrounding soil and accumulate to high concentrations. Exposure to high concentrations can pose significant health risks. As a result, I will also be developing accessible and interactive cartographic applications to not only better communicate the risk of radon in general, but also to help the public identify which areas of the province are at a higher risk for exposure.

  • Ben Jestico, MSc Candidate
    BSc(University of Victoria)

    Spatial analysis of cycling patterns in Victoria, BC

    My research interests lie in understanding the variables that influence cycling behaviour in Victoria, BC. Additionally, I am interested in publically sourced information and how it can be incorporated within academic research. My MSc will utilize publically sourced information through an online platform for reporting cycling incidents in the Capital Regional District (CRD). These data, combined with additional sources, will help identify cycling behaviour and potentially high risk areas that can inform policy related to cycling in urban environments.

  • Gillian Harvey, MSc Candidate
    BSc(University of Victoria)

    Spatial analysis of marine mammals in British Columbia

    I am passionate about marine conservation and coastal management in my home province of British Columbia. I am intrigued by how spatial analysis can be used to increase our understanding of the complex dynamics between humans and marine wildlife. In collaboration with the Raincoast Conservation Foundation, my M.Sc. research aims to model the distribution and density of 11 marine mammal species across coastal BC to identify regions of critical habitat. My work will also generate a spatial assessment of marine mammal risk to a variety of anthropogenic hazards to assess conservation concerns and inform management decisions in BC.

  • Darren Boss, MSc Candidate
    BSc(University of Victoria)

    Spatial analysis of cycling incidents in the Capital Regional District

    My research interests relate to the spatial analysis of cycling incidents and hazards reported by members of the cycling community. My long-term goals include informing policy related to infrastructure development and the promotion of cycling as a healthy and safe alternative means of transportation. As a long time cycle commuter, I am keenly aware of the challenges cyclists face in an urban environment and the historical lack of options for reporting hazards and incidents. I am interested in the development of user friendly platforms for gathering such volunteer geographic information from the cycling community. I am currently involved in the development of a mobile app that will be used to report hazards, visualize reports from other cyclists and provide real-time notification of reported hazards.

SPAR Staff

  • Wiebe Nijland, Hakai Research Postdoctoral Fellow
    PhD (Utrecht University)

    Wiebe Nijland received his PhD in Earth Sciences (2011) from Utrecht University in The Netherlands, his motherland. His main research interests are in the detection of ecosystem functioning and the use of spatial and temporal patterns to understand landscape processes. He uses remote- and proximal sensing technology to bridge a wide range process scales, supported by modeling and spatial statistics. Before coming to the Islands he worked as a postdoc in Nicholas Coops' IRSS lab at the Forestry faculty of UBC, mapping Grizzly Bear habitat and vegetation structure from Landsat, lidar, and camera networks. Wiebe is an enthusiastic photographer in visible wavelengths and beyond. He also likes to bike and enjoys the outdoors both above water and under, taking advantage of any opportunity to scubadive and photographing the underwater realm.
